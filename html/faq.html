General Questions

How do I know what kind of batteries I have?

Fortunately, you can figure out the type of battery you have by checking in one of these locations: The battery case usually has the name and number of the manufacturer of the battery. If you look closely at your battery, you will probably find the type of battery it is. The label on the battery will tell you what kind of battery it is. Label information is usually more focused on promoting a product rather than providing useful details. Due to this, identifying the indicated battery type on the label might be difficult. Please send us pictures of your batteries with any markings on them, part numbers, stickers, any information that you have about where batteries came from, etc. There are many battery types that are dangerous to handle or ship. Please feel free to contact us if you have any questions or concerns.

How can a car battery be disposed of properly?

After using your car for a while, you will notice that its battery is not as good as when you first got it. To keep your car running smoothly for a long time, it's best to get a new battery if your current one is getting old. However, getting rid of your old batteries is a different matter. It's really important to understand where to get rid of car batteries the right way. Regular cars and trucks have batteries that contain harmful heavy metals and toxic chemicals, unlike electric vehicles. When batteries are not disposed of correctly, the fluids inside them can leak out and pollute the area around them. Car batteries must be disposed of responsibly, and they cannot be simply thrown out with other trash. Depending on the quantity you have, we can assist you and provide you with remedies for how to make recycling of your car battery safe and responsible.

What is your responsible approach to recycle the batteries?

Different batteries are made up of different parts. These parts are made of different materials, such as lead, lithium-ion, nickel-metal hydride, nickel-cadmium, lithium-ion polymer, nickel-zinc, and alkaline. Producers use these parts to make various types of batteries. These batteries can be different in how they are used, how much energy they have, and how they can be recycled. Each type of battery is recycled in its own way. Usually, the outside covering of the battery is taken off or taken apart first. The different parts are organized by category, cleaned and treated, and then put back into the new materials for making products. The battery is smashed into pieces by a machine called a hammer mill. After the battery breaks, its pieces are put into a container. In that container, the heavy stuff and lead sink down, while the plastic stays on top. At this stage, the polypropylene pieces are removed and the liquids are taken out, leaving only the lead and heavy metals. Each material is placed into a separate recycling category.

What’s the proper way to package lithium/NiMH batteries?

Proper packaging of lithium batteries is crucial to prevent any electrical incidents from occurring. One way to ensure the protection of the ends is by covering them with tape or shrink wrap. It is essential to have the correct labels on the package in order to effectively caution and instruct individuals regarding its handling. It is necessary to use robust cardboard boxes for the batteries. These boxes must be taped closed and stacked safely on strong pallets. After placing the pallets, they need to be tightly wrapped with stretch film to ensure their secure transportation.

What’s the proper way to package sealed lead acid batteries?

If your lead acid battery doesn't start an engine, it doesn't mean it's completely dead. If you touch the wrong parts together, it could make things really hot, blow up, or start a fire. Sealed Lead Acid batteries that use Gel or AGM technology are generally considered non-spillable. However, even a "sealed" standard lead acid battery with liquid electrolyte can still spill. It is very important to understand what 'non-spillable' means. A battery that is sealed does not always mean that it won't spill. In the US, a "non-spillable battery" is defined as a battery that doesn't have any liquid that can flow out and the liquid inside the battery should not leak out if the case is cracked at a temperature of 55°C (131°F). Non-spillable lead acid batteries, which use Gel or Absorbent Glass Matt technology, need to be packaged similarly to batteries filled with acid. However, there are a few differences. You don't need a liner that can withstand acid. The box needs to have a clear label that says "non-spillable battery. " You can use labels, but they just need to be clearly marked and long-lasting, according to the IATA. Please be aware that even though this is a rule set by IATA, many companies that transport goods by sea and land also require it. DOT road safety rules must be followed when packaging VRLA batteries. They should be placed on strong pallets and separated by cardboard sheets between each layer and on top. It is suggested to use stretch wrap to hold the batteries in place on the pallet.

What’s the proper way to package absolyte batteries?

When moving or carrying batteries, it is important to keep the ends facing upwards to avoid touching any metal. This can prevent any sparks in case the battery is still charged. This might start a fire or give you an electric shock. Safety regulations must be followed when packaging Absolyte batteries for transportation on roads. The batteries should be positioned on robust pallets, with cardboard sheets separating each layer and placed on the top as well. Ensure the batteries are safely fixed to the pallet by utilizing stretch wrap.
